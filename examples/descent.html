The Descent

Sunlight fails at 200 metres. Below it lies the largest, least-explored habitat on Earth. Scroll to fall 10,935 metres — past the whales, past the wrecks, past an inverted Everest — to the deepest point in the ocean.

More people have stood on the Moon than have reached the bottom of the Challenger Deep. We have mapped the surface of Mars in finer detail than our own sea floor.

The ocean averages 3,700 metres deep and holds 97% of Earth's water. Everything below is dark, cold, and under crushing pressure — yet it teems with life. Here is what the journey down actually looks like.

Living light

In the dark, ~90% of animals glow.

Bioluminescence is the most common form of communication on Earth. Cut the sunlight and the deep sea is not empty — it sparks. Turn it off, then on.

Every point of light is a real behaviour: a counter-illuminating belly, an anglerfish lure, a startled jelly firing its whole body at once.

The crush

At the floor of the Challenger Deep the water presses down with the weight of about 100 African elephants standing on your head — roughly 8 tonnes on every square inch.

A cup of polystyrene sent to the bottom returns the size of a thimble, permanently. Life survives it only by having no air spaces to crush: bodies flooded with water and pressure-resistant molecules called piezolytes.
